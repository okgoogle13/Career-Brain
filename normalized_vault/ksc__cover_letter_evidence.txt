# Cover Letter Evidence Examples

## 1. Passion for Peer-Based, Drug-Related Harm Reduction and Health Promotion

### **Opening Story - Career Pivot:**
*"In 2018, I made a decision that surprised my colleagues but felt inevitable to me. I walked away from a lucrative Senior Project Manager role in London's financial district to become a volunteer peer worker in Melbourne. This wasn't a career change—it was a homecoming. Having navigated my own journey with substance use and experienced firsthand the transformative power of non-judgmental peer support, I knew where my true contribution lay."*

### **Sustained Commitment Evidence:**
*"Over the past 6 years, I've consistently chosen roles that align with harm reduction principles, even when other opportunities offered better pay or status. From delivering 2,000+ care packages during COVID isolation to developing culturally sensitive mental health resources for international students, I've sought ways to reduce barriers and stigma at every opportunity."*

### **Philosophy in Action:**
*"My approach to harm reduction isn't just professional—it's personal. When I facilitated bi-weekly peer support groups, I didn't just share evidence-based strategies; I shared the lived reality of what it means to navigate services while experiencing stigma. This dual perspective allowed me to create genuinely safe spaces where clients felt truly understood, not just helped."*

## 2. Lived or Living Experience of Substance Use

### **Current, Ongoing Experience:**
*"My lived experience isn't historical—it's ongoing and evolving. As a current participant in novel psychoactive substance trials for treatment-resistant depression, I understand both the therapeutic potential and complex realities of substance use within medical frameworks. This experience provides me with authentic peer connection and real-time insight into the questions and concerns people bring to drug checking services."*

### **Bridge Between Seeking and Providing Support:**
*"I've sat on both sides of the peer support relationship. I know what it feels like to approach a service wondering if you'll be judged, dismissed, or pathologized. I also know the profound impact of being met with genuine understanding and practical, non-judgmental support. This dual perspective allows me to anticipate client needs and respond with authentic empathy."*

### **Professional Integration:**
*"My lived experience isn't separate from my professional competence—it's integral to it. Through SMART Recovery facilitator training and ongoing therapeutic relationships, I've learned to leverage personal experience while maintaining appropriate boundaries. This means I can offer genuine peer connection without compromising professional effectiveness."*

## 3. Knowledge of Drug Markets, Trends and Emerging Novel Psychoactive Substances

### **Real-Time, Supervised Learning:**
*"Through my current participation in novel psychoactive substance trials, I'm gaining supervised, real-time experience with emerging compounds that many peer workers only encounter through client reports. This provides me with current, evidence-based understanding of how these substances work, their potential interactions, and their effects—knowledge that's invaluable when explaining test results or discussing risk reduction strategies."*

### **Community + Clinical Perspective:**
*"My understanding of drug markets and trends comes from multiple sources: community engagement through peer work, personal navigation of both therapeutic and recreational contexts, and ongoing exposure to cutting-edge research through trial participation. This integrated perspective means I understand not just what substances do, but why people use them, how they access them, and what concerns they bring to drug checking services."*

### **Practical Application Examples:**
*"During my time facilitating peer support groups, I regularly helped clients understand poly-substance interactions, medication contraindications, and harm reduction strategies for substances they were encountering in community. My combination of lived experience and ongoing therapeutic substance use allowed me to provide nuanced, practical guidance that purely academic knowledge couldn't match."*

## **Powerful Integration Statements:**

### **For Passion:**
*"My commitment to harm reduction isn't just philosophical—it's the reason I restructured my entire career and life around this work."*

### **For Lived Experience:**
*"I bring not just historical lived experience, but current, ongoing understanding of substance use within both therapeutic and community contexts."*  

### **For Knowledge:**
*"My knowledge of emerging substances comes from the unique combination of supervised clinical exposure, community peer work, and personal navigation of both therapeutic and recreational contexts."*

## **Story Structure Examples:**

### **Challenge-Action-Result Format:**
*"When a client in my peer support group expressed anxiety about potential interactions between their prescribed medication and substances they were using recreationally (Challenge), I drew from both my lived experience and trial participation to help them understand the pharmacokinetics involved, connect them with appropriate medical support, and develop practical harm reduction strategies (Action). They later told me it was the first time a support worker had discussed their concerns without judgment or oversimplification, and they felt empowered to make informed decisions about their use (Result)."*

### **Values-Driven Decision Making:**
*"When I learned about HRVic's drug checking service, I immediately recognized it as the kind of evidence-based, non-judgmental intervention I've been passionate about throughout my career transition. This represents exactly why I left finance—to contribute to services that treat people who use drugs with dignity and provide them with the information they need to make safer choices."*
